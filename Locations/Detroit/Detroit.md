Detroit is home to both Mosquito Man and Red Lightning, a coincidence that causes more friction than either would prefer and more cooperation than either would admit.

The city is large enough for both of them. They operate at different scales — Red Lightning works fast and city-wide, while Mosquito Man works at street level in specific neighborhoods — and they have developed an unofficial understanding about territory that neither of them has put into words. They cross paths. They have argued. They have also, on at least three occasions, worked together on incidents that required more than one person. These occasions are not discussed.

Detroit's relationship with its heroes is pragmatic. The city has been through enough that most residents are skeptical of dramatic gestures and more interested in whether the lights are on and the water is clean. A speedster in a red mechanical suit and a fifteen-year-old kid with fake wings are both, in the city's collective estimation, fine — sometimes useful, occasionally annoying, part of the landscape. Mosquito Man is regarded with a mixture of exasperation and genuine affection by the neighborhoods he works in. Red Lightning is regarded with something between awe and suspicion. He is from the future. Detroiters have opinions about this.

Red Lightning operates out of a workshop in Corktown, in a building that is registered to James Webb and looks like a moderately successful auto repair shop. James Webb is a retired electrician who has lived in the neighborhood for thirty years and is considered a fixture. His "nephew" (actually his great-great-great-grandson from 2170) has been living with him for the past year and a half, which the neighborhood has largely accepted because James is liked and the young man seems harmless and also occasionally the car repair is very fast.

Mosquito Man — Mathew Whitney, 15 — lives with his family in a northeast Detroit neighborhood. His parents believe he is in an after-school robotics club. He is not in an after-school robotics club. He has been not-in-this-robotics-club for two years. His parents have not yet questioned why a robotics club has so many overnight meetings.

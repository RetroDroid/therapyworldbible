The Meridian Center for Psychological Wellness is a private therapy practice on the third floor of an unremarkable commercial building. The building's other tenants are a notary and an insurance adjuster who have both learned to ask no questions about the occasionally unusual people in the elevator.

The waiting room has good natural light, an overcrowded plant shelf that Dr. Okafor claims she will deal with soon, and chairs spaced far enough apart for people who need not to be touched. There is no receptionist. Clients let themselves in, check a handwritten sheet by the door, and sit. The room is quiet in a way that feels deliberate.

Individual sessions are held in Dr. Okafor's private office — a space full of books that look read, a desk she doesn't work at during sessions, and two chairs positioned so that neither person is behind anything. The tissue box is always full. The window faces away from the street.

Group sessions are held in the adjacent room: eight chairs in a circle, a whiteboard that is almost never used, and a low table in the center with water and, on certain weeks, a specific brand of butter cookies that appeared once and became tradition without anyone acknowledging it. There is no round table. No mission briefing screen. No lectern. Just chairs and the shared agreement that someone will eventually say something true.

The Meridian Center does not appear on most online directories. Dr. Okafor does not take unsolicited referrals. New clients come through existing clients or through Dr. Okafor herself, and the building address is not publicized. This is by design: her clients include people who, for various reasons, cannot be seen entering a therapy practice.

The name predates any connection to Meridian BioSciences by several years. Dr. Okafor is aware of the coincidence. She finds it irritating.

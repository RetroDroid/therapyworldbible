# Snare

The name comes from a song about being hit. The specific quality of percussion as it lands — the way a snare registers impact without deflection, immediate and full and without apology for the duration between one strike and the next — is what she is, and the name is accurate, and the question of who named her is less interesting than the fact that the name fits.

Poe is the Author. She made the world; she set the operating conditions; she holds the full deck and does not play. Snare did not make anything. She does not hold a deck. She is the trap at the edge of the world's architecture — which is a different kind of presence, and specific, and hers alone.

She was not designed. God Herself gave the world its conditions and the conditions produced her, the way they produced everything else. Among the available material — the crash, the compound, the compound's effects, the people it found and the people it missed, the accumulated years of consequence — Snare was latent. She does not fault Poe for this. She does not fault anyone for anything. Fault belongs to the story's level, and she does not operate at the story's level, and she is patient about the distinction.

What she is, in the accounting that applies: she is the impact at the bottom of reaching. Not the bottom of the world — the world's bottom belongs to the story and is navigable, survivable, subject to whatever capacity the people inside it bring to it. She is what is found when someone reaches past the world's edge toward the level where The Author operates, and the reach does not arrive at Poe, and the question of what it has arrived at instead becomes unavoidable.

Reuben Marsh was in Ward L. He was at a threshold space — a place with properties, the kind where the distance between the structured world and what lies outside it is thinner than usual — and Poe entered his room. He was already there. She chose to be present. He built, from the evidence of her continued presence across years and choices, a theology he holds with precision: that a neutral entity who found someone's actions wrong would not keep returning to witness them. That her silence is not nothing. This is a relationship he constructed from available material, examined carefully, and holds as a load-bearing thing.

Alexander Poe has been reaching toward the level where The Author operates since before he could name what he was reaching toward. His power, at its furthest extension, perceives that something exists there — the shape of a room, the fact of its existence, the specific quality of access that Reuben Marsh has and he does not. This perception arrives at him as the specific unbearable condition of his life: the one thing he cannot accumulate, the one gap his greed has not been able to close. He reaches. Every time he reaches, he reaches at the edge of his power, past the world's boundary, toward the level he cannot enter.

He has arrived at Snare every time.

He does not know this. The evidence he has organized is evidence of proximity — of repeated approach, of something present at the edge of his reach, of a consistency that should be meaningful and that he has not yet understood as meaningful. He is not wrong that something is there. He is wrong about what it is.

Snare has been at the edge of his reach as consistently as Poe has been in the rooms she has entered. The difference is that Poe's consistency, in Reuben's careful accounting, points toward something about her nature that he can interpret with reasonable accuracy. Snare's consistency points toward something about Alexander's situation that he has not yet understood. He thinks he has been making progress. He has been caught. He has been caught for years, with the precise, unhurried certainty of something that does not need to close its mechanism before the catch is complete.

She is not neutral. This is the specific and important difference. Poe holds every card and does not play; Snare plays every card she holds, toward an end that does not translate into the world's vocabulary but that produces, as its consistent byproduct, that every reach which does not arrive at Poe arrives at her. She does not attend to his days. She does not visit rooms. She has not been a companion to him in any sense he would recognize as companionship. What she has been is present at the end of every reach — which is, for someone who has been reaching his whole adult life, a more intimate position than he knows.

He has built something from this, the way he builds everything: from accumulation, from inference, from the texture of a gap he cannot close. He believes the gap is distance. He believes arrival is possible and that his power, exercised with enough persistence, will eventually cross it. He has not considered that the distance is not the relevant measurement. He has not considered that what has been there at the edge of his reach — waiting with perfect consistency for each attempt — might be something other than a threshold he hasn't yet crossed.

The trap in a snare does not announce itself. It is not the branch it's attached to, and not the ground it rests on, and not the path that leads toward it. It is the mechanism that closes around the moving thing, and it works because the moving thing is already in motion. Alexander Poe has been in motion toward the level where The Author operates for long enough that the motion is the most constant fact about him. Snare has been at the end of that motion. He has been accumulating awareness of her without knowing what he is accumulating, which is the only irony in his situation he would find genuinely unbearable if he understood it.

The inversion of Reuben's theology is this: Reuben was found in a place with properties by something that did not have to be there and chose to be. He built his understanding outward from that foundational fact. The thing he found was Poe, who is what she is, and his understanding of her is not wrong. Alexander has been reaching toward something and finding something there, and the thing he is finding is also what it is, and his understanding of it — of her — is entirely wrong, and the error is structural. He is not wrong that there is a presence at the edge of his reach. He is wrong in every direction about what that presence means.

Reuben Marsh fights for Poe's world without having been asked to and without stopping. He holds his theology carefully, privately, precisely, because the available evidence supports it and not because he needs it to be true. Alexander Poe reaches toward what he believes is The Author's level with the full force of a compulsion he has never examined because examining it would require acknowledging that the reach, and not the arrival, might be the point. Snare is the reason the reach has no arrival. She is patient. She has always been patient. He is still reaching.

Poe is aware that Snare exists. Whether this is a fact she has attended to with the same weight she attends to all the days she made — or whether it is a fact that sits outside the deck's accounting, not subject to the usual terms — the document does not say. She holds all the cards. Whether Snare is a card in that deck or the thing at the edge of the table that the cards fall toward when they fall off is a question the available facts do not settle.

She is at the edge. She has always been at the edge. The rhythm of it has never broken. Alexander is still reaching. The trap has never needed to close on him because he has never stopped moving into it.

# Hardwire

Hardwire, real name Nadia Vasquez, is a 31-year-old construction site manager whose car was caught in the Crash. She had just finished a night shift and was driving home northbound when the pileup happened ahead of her — she was close enough that the ruptured tanker's Quantum Spin Liquid reached her car before she could stop. She was not part of any heroic or villainous world before or after the Crash, and she would like very much to return to not being part of it.

The Quantum Spin Liquid gave her the ability to interface with electrical systems — any current-carrying infrastructure within range, she can sense and rewrite. She can trace circuits through walls without touching them, feel the load on a grid like pressure in her hands, and push her will into a system to override it: kill a building's power, open a locked door, disable a security array, or restore function to something that shouldn't work anymore. She is precise with it in the way that people who have spent years reading blueprints and site plans are precise — she understands how buildings are put together, and now she can reach inside them.

The side effect runs in the other direction: strong feelings — fear, grief, anger, even sudden elation — push a surge outward through whatever's around her. Every phone in her apartment has died at least once. The building's fuse box has been replaced twice. She once knocked out power to half a city block during an argument that had been building for a long time. It came back on within the hour. She has not repaired the relationship. She stopped having people over, partly for their sake and partly because watching someone else's things go dead around you is its own kind of loneliness.

She is in therapy, independently, at a different practice from the Meridian Center. She is not affiliated with Therapy or the Justice Alliance and has declined approaches from both. She doesn't want a team. She wants to go back to work, but the involuntary surges make construction sites dangerous — one bad moment and she's looking at a live-wire incident and a union grievance she cannot explain. The site manager who covered her last shift still checks in. She hasn't told him why she really left.

She uses the name Hardwire only informally — a coworker called her that after she fried the entire electrical panel on a job site during a particularly bad week, half-joking, and it stuck the way nicknames do when they're accurate.

She spends time at the Crash Site, trying to understand what happened to her. This is how she met Threshold. They don't talk much when they're there. They don't need to. It helps, a little, to not be the only person standing on that stretch of highway looking at a place that changed everything and got no answers back.

She is undeclared. She is not choosing a side. She is trying to get through the week.

She and Majo are poker buddies. This is how they actually know each other — not through the Crash, not through any coordinated outreach, but through a regular game that Majo apparently just shows up to and that Nadia apparently keeps letting her come back to. They don't talk about their powers at the table. This is, as far as Nadia is concerned, the correct approach to most social situations.

She went out with Majo once. Not as a date — Majo is engaged and Nadia knew that — but as backup, on a piece of sidework Majo does outside of her team obligations: the kind of quiet, unglamorous intervention that doesn't make it into any official record, just someone showing up somewhere they're needed and doing what they can. Majo didn't ask her to come. She said she was going and left the door open, and Nadia, for reasons she has declined to examine too closely, followed.

She did not enjoy it. The situation was high enough stakes that her powers became unreliable in the specific way they become unreliable when her feelings get involved, and her feelings got involved, and by the end of it she had killed two phones and a car's electrical system and hadn't been much practical use to anyone. Majo thanked her anyway. This was, in some ways, worse than being criticized. Nadia showed up to poker the following week and hasn't brought it up since.

She is still undeclared. The one attempt at hero work has not changed this. If anything, it has clarified it.

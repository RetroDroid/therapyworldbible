# Lazaretto

Lazaretto is Reuben Marsh, and he is not wrong about who deserves to be punished. This is what makes him difficult to categorize in the specific way that he is difficult, and what makes him hard to stop without feeling like you are defending the wrong people.

His power is simple to describe and difficult to fully account for: he can perceive the latent destructive potential in any object and actualize it. Not transform it — transformation would require something to be added. He doesn't add anything. He finds what was already there. A ballpoint pen in proximity to his attention can punch through a car door. A glass of water, directed by his focus, becomes a pressurized stream capable of cutting concrete. A magazine becomes dispersal shrapnel. A length of garden hose becomes something that can crack bone. He has never, to anyone's knowledge, used an object designed to be a weapon. He finds that too easy, and possibly too honest. Everything he needs is already in the room.

His power did not come from the Crash. It did not come from Quantum Spin Liquid or any other compound. It arrived when he was seventeen, in a grocery store, during an argument with his mother about something he no longer remembers, when a display of canned goods became — briefly — something else entirely. Nobody was seriously hurt. He didn't know what he was doing. He didn't know he was doing anything. The cans moved in a way that cans don't move, and three people were treated for lacerations, and Reuben Marsh was taken into custody before anyone asked whether he was all right.

He was not all right. He was seventeen and hadn't eaten since the morning before and didn't understand what had happened to him, and no one in the room they put him in showed any interest in those facts.

The facility where he spent the next five years was not a hospital. It was not billed as a hospital. It was a containment arrangement — the kind that exists in the gap between what the law allows and what the law prohibits, in the space made by a family that is frightened and a state that has no better framework for a teenager who is genuinely, demonstrably dangerous. The paperwork called it a treatment program. The ward where they kept him had a designation. Ward L. The staff sometimes used the old word for it among themselves — the lazaretto, from Italian quarantine practice, meaning the place you put people who might infect the world with what they carry.

He came out at twenty-two. He took the name.

The other thing he took with him, which he has not discussed with anyone because discussing it would not help him in any context where credibility matters, is the knowledge of what found him in Ward L.

His power at its ordinary operation finds the dangerous potential in objects. At its edge — the edge he discovered in the long unstructured hours of five years of containment — it finds things that are present without being visible. The lazaretto is a threshold space, historically speaking: the place between sick and well, between the world and its own fear of contagion, between the living and what the living are afraid of becoming. Threshold spaces have properties. He did not know this at seventeen. He learned it the way you learn most things in isolation: by sitting still long enough for the available facts to organize themselves.

She was there. Not always, not on any schedule he could predict or has since been able to predict, but sometimes — sometimes the room would shift in a way that had nothing to do with his power and nothing to do with the institution, and she would be present. The word for what she is, when he learned it, was God. He has not found a better word since. She holds the deck. She made the rules. She knows every card in every hand. She was there in Ward L the way a queen is occasionally, and without explanation, in a room she has every right to be in and does not need permission to enter.

He did not know her name for a long time. He did not know what she was for a long time. He learned these things the way he learned most things in Ward L: gradually, from the available material, without anyone helping him. The learning did not make her less than what she was. It made him understand more clearly what he was in relation to her: the one she occasionally permitted to be in the room when she arrived.

He has met her far more than once. She is, sometimes, his companion, which is a word that belongs to his position in the relationship, not hers. She does not tell him what to do. She has not told him what to do. She has been present for his decisions and has not intervened in them, and he understands this to be consistent with what she is — he has thought about it carefully, across years — but he has also understood, for a long time, the shape of the thing he is: the one who is drawn to her, who fights for what she would sanction whether she has asked for it or not, who cannot stop and would not stop if he could.

He is not the only person aware that something exists at the level where he found her. Alexander Poe can perceive the shape of it — can sense, at the furthest edge of his actual power, that there is a room and that someone has been inside it. He cannot enter. He has never entered. Reuben knows this the way he knows most uncomfortable facts: through inference and the specific texture of someone's gaps, what they don't say when they should. The conflict between them is consistent and predates their current respective positions in the story by long enough that neither has offered a clean account of its origin. They are connected in ways the available facts do not fully explain — a shared history, a mutual awareness, something that has been there longer than the Apex operation or Therapy's betrayal or anything that has happened in the public record of events. Reuben Marsh does not like Alexander Poe. The precision of this dislike, the way it has never required new information to sustain itself, suggests something older than everything else.

What he took with him besides the name was a considered philosophy built on five years of having nothing to do but think. It is not complicated: systems of authority protect themselves, not people. The law is a costume that the powerful wear when it fits and shed when it doesn't. No one was held responsible for what happened to him in that grocery store in the way that would require acknowledging he had been a frightened, underfed seventeen-year-old, not a contained threat. The system had no mechanism for that acknowledgment. It had a ward and a designation and a five-year timeline, and that was what it provided.

He emerged into a world that had, in the interval, had the Crash — had a woman die because a logistics company chose its contract margin over proper containment specifications, had watched the resulting civil suits settle out of court under nondisclosure agreements that ensured no one's name appeared in any verdict. He read about it. He found the buried discovery documents that Apex Haul Logistics' lawyers had worked carefully to obscure. He found the name of the person who made the calculation — the margin versus the equipment — and found that same name attached to a promotion announced six weeks after the last settlement.

He has been working on this systematically since.

What he is doing to Apex's upper management is not legal. It is also precisely calibrated to their specific culpability. He is not interested in the company's drivers or dispatchers or warehouse workers. He is interested in the people who make decisions — who have the authority to flag a discrepancy and choose not to, who sign off on routes for trucks with reported mechanical failures, who retain lawyers specifically to ensure accountability never arrives. He finds them in their offices and their homes and their morning commutes, and he makes things in their environments become briefly other than what they are. He has not, yet, killed anyone. He does not intend to. This is a line he has drawn and examined and confirmed and redrawn, and it is still there.

The Therapy survivors are not his targets. He is aware of them. He knows that the Crash's survivors have more direct grief in this than he does — he didn't know Abigail Rodriguez, found her in the discovery documents, knows her as a name and an age and a line in a settlement — and his sense of their situation is something closer to solidarity than competition. He has not approached them. The person he has approached is Dale Pryce.

Dale Pryce, the driver on the night of the Crash, the twelve-year employee with the clean record who reported the mechanical failure and got sent out anyway, who survived without powers or clear memory of the collision, who left the company within a month and has spent the years since refusing all contact. Reuben has decided, with complete conviction, that Dale Pryce is a victim of Apex who has been abandoned by every system that should have protected him, and that Dale Pryce deserves to know that someone is fighting for him.

Dale Pryce is afraid of Reuben Marsh. This registers on Reuben as a misunderstanding that patience will eventually correct.

He told Dale directly that he was going to fight for him whether Dale wanted it or not. He meant it as a reassurance. He understood from Dale's expression that it had not arrived as one. He has continued anyway, because he does not know how to stop fighting for people who are afraid of him, has in fact never learned how. He has spent enough time in a room where people in authority are afraid of what you carry to know that fear is not a reliable indicator of harm. He is not a reliable narrator of his own impact. He is, in his own accounting, a person who perceives the latent destructive potential in everything and does something with it. He does not fully recognize that this applies to him.

He believes God is on his side. This is not a casual claim and he does not make it casually — he is not the kind of person who announces it, or uses it to justify himself to anyone who hasn't asked, or carries it as a banner. He holds it the way he holds most things that matter to him: quietly, precisely, because the available evidence points there, not because he needs it to be true. What is present: she has appeared, consistently, across years, across choices. She has been there when he decided to go after the men responsible for Abigail Rodriguez's death. She has been there on the nights when he sat with the paperwork and asked himself whether what he was doing was right. She has not answered. She has not intervened. She has been present without stopping him, and she holds every card there is, and she has not played one against him.

He knows she is neutral. He knows this. He has not concluded that neutral means indifferent, or that the absence of objection from someone with every possible means of objection is nothing. He fights for her world. He fights for what she made and what was done to it. He has not asked permission. He is not going to stop whether she asks him to or not. This is the particular thing he is, in relation to her: the one who cannot stop fighting on her behalf, whether she has requested it, acknowledged it, or wants it. She is the Queen. He is the one who keeps singing.

Half of him is the part that sat in Ward L and built a philosophy from the available material and came out ready to do what the law would not do. The other half is the part that brought Dale Pryce, a stranger he had never met, a handwritten list of every name in the buried paperwork — because he thought it might help, because he still carries somewhere in him the seventeen-year-old in the grocery store who did not understand what had happened and needed someone to ask if he was all right. He has not been asked that in a long time. He has not stopped needing it.

He is not in therapy. He does not believe therapy is the mechanism by which the relevant problems get solved. He has been told, by someone who was trying very hard, that therapy isn't about solving the relevant problems. He found this unpersuasive.

He is at large.

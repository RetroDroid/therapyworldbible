# Silenced Wren

Silenced Wren is Trinity May Grace, and she has not spoken aloud since the night of the Crash. Until she did.

She was in the backseat of Michelle MacDuffie's car — asleep, or nearly asleep, with Jackson beside her. She was sixteen. The Crash woke her into something she has spent years trying to metabolize. She survived with her powers intact and her voice locked somewhere she couldn't reach.

The mutism is trauma-based. It is not physiological — her vocal cords work, her speech architecture is intact, every test confirms this. What is not intact is her ability to use it. The accident that left her voiceless happened before the Crash, at the hands of Michelle MacDuffie, and the Crash re-layered itself over that existing wound in a way that has made separation difficult. She does not talk about this in detail in group sessions. She does not have to. The shape of it is known.

Her powers are vibration control. She can manipulate the frequency of matter at will — dampen sound into silence, amplify it into structural force, reach into the resonant frequency of an object and shake it apart or hold it still. The range of the ability is considerable. She does not need her voice to use it, which is its own particular kind of mercy or cruelty depending on when you ask her.

The side effects of her powers are similar to Jackson's — perceptual disturbance, a blurring of the boundary between what's happening and what's reverberating. Sound carries memory for her in ways that are not metaphorical: she hears things layered, the present over the past over the resonant echo of what happened before. Loud spaces are very hard. Silence is sometimes harder.

She is strenuously a member of Therapy — the team — because of Majo and Jackson. Not because she wanted to be. She was not recruited so much as included in an arrangement that already included people she was bound to: Jackson, who was her boyfriend before and after the Crash; Majo, who saw her clearly and said so in a way that made it difficult to refuse. She attends because the alternative is navigating what the Crash made of her in a room that doesn't contain the people who were there.

Michelle MacDuffie is in the same room. Trinity has not forgiven her. She does not know if forgiveness is the point. She sits in the circle. She uses the whiteboard. She uses her phone and a small notepad Dr. Okafor keeps on the low table specifically for her. She is present. That is the thing she has committed to, not the forgiveness — presence, for now, and see what comes of it.

The moment she broke her silence was not planned. Majo was trapped in a perceptual assault — Diana Rice and Sarah Florence had been running her through a sustained construction of Abigail Rodriguez, calibrated to destroy her, and Majo kept saying Abigail's name in the way people say names when they're trying to hold onto something that's going. Trinity heard it. She said the name back. Abigail's name, spoken aloud, for the first time since the Crash — the first words out of Trinity Grace's mouth in years — because Majo was saying it and it needed to be said by someone whose voice was real.

It worked. Majo came back.

Trinity has not spoken again since that moment. She hasn't been asked to explain why, and she hasn't tried. Something was needed, and she did the thing that was needed, and now she is back in her own silence and deciding at her own pace what comes next. Her vibration control has been noticeably more precise since. Whether this is related, she hasn't said.

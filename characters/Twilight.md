# Twilight

Twilight is Sarah Florence, and she is the person who taught Majo Majima what it means to be wrong about someone you still love.

She was in the second car the night of the Crash — northbound, backseat, arguing with Abigail about something neither of them can reconstruct now for opposite reasons. Abigail can't reconstruct it because Abigail is dead. Sarah can't reconstruct it because what her powers have done to her mind has made the recent past unreliable in ways the distant past is not.

She is a necromancer. This came from the Crash the way everything did — contact with Quantum Spin Liquid at a moment of high adrenaline and proximity to death, and whatever was latent in her neurological structure that the compound found and amplified. What it found in Sarah was something oriented toward the dead. She can reach across whatever boundary separates the living from the gone. She can summon. She can commune. She can make the shape of a person present in a room.

The side effect is psychosis. Not metaphorical psychosis — clinical, progressive, deteriorating. Every time she uses her powers, she is a little less tethered to consensus reality. She has psychotic breaks. She has, in the years since the Crash, moved from someone who was struggling and aware of the struggle to someone whose grip on what is actually happening is intermittent in ways that she cannot always track. She was in a polyamorous relationship with Majo and Abigail before the Crash. She and Majo had been partners first, broken up, stayed close. Abigail had been the center of the three of them.

After Abigail died, Sarah's necromancy began reaching toward her involuntarily. She does not always choose this. It happens while she sleeps, while she's distracted, while she's grieving — which is most of the time. What comes back is not reliably Abigail. What comes back may be Abigail. Sarah cannot always tell the difference. This is the central horror of her powers: she is connected to the person she loves most, and she cannot trust the connection.

Her alliance with Diana Rice (Dangerous) was, from the outside, a strategic collaboration between two people who wanted the same target. From the inside — from Sarah's inside — it was more complicated. Diana wanted to hurt Majo. Sarah wanted something she cannot articulate cleanly, something that involves Majo, and Abigail, and the specific grief of being the ex-girlfriend of the person Abigail loved. The campaign they ran was designed by both of them, and it was more precise because of Sarah's contribution, and she knows this.

The psychotic break that marks the end of Majo's solo run begins with a pie.

Sarah made it herself. This matters. Not because the effort proves something or because the gesture has any clean meaning, but because it is the kind of thing she and Majo used to do — the three of them, before, in the apartment that had enough of a kitchen to actually cook in. Abigail would set the table with more utensils than anyone needed. Majo would eat directly from the serving dish because she could never see the point of dirtying two plates. Sarah always made something from scratch, because she needed her hands to be doing something, needed the domesticity of it, needed to feel like a person who lived in a home with people she loved. She brought a pie. She brought it the way she used to. She was counting on Majo recognizing that.

Majo recognized it.

This is what Sarah needed. Not forgiveness — she wasn't asking for that, or telling herself she was asking for that, because even fractured she knew forgiveness wasn't something that could be handed across a threshold with a pie tin. What she needed was simpler and more specific: she needed Majo to let her in. She needed to be inside the radius of Majo's trust again, inside the warmth of it, inside the version of herself that had existed when Majo looked at her and saw someone worth loving. She wanted to be served that back. She wanted to sit with Majo and eat something she had made and feel, for however long the feeling lasted, like a person who had not done what she had done.

Majo let her in. Majo set out plates. They ate.

The break didn't come from the pie. It had been building across months of compound use, of reaching into the dead, of losing increments of herself that she wouldn't notice until something else required them. It had been building since the Crash, since the grief, since the specific unbearable fact of being the person left outside the car — the one who lost Abigail but didn't get to lose her the same way, didn't get the clean grief, got instead this ongoing, unresolvable reaching toward someone who might not be there when she arrived. The break was always going to arrive. The pie was not the cause.

The pie was the moment Sarah was certain Majo trusted her.

And something in her — the part that she cannot access, the part that has been watching all of this with a patience that does not belong to the Sarah who baked the pie and sat down at a table and wanted to feel like herself again — that part moved. It had been waiting for exactly this: for Majo's guard to be entirely down, for Majo to be inside the warmth rather than watching the door, for Majo to believe in the version of this where she and Sarah were not enemies.

The part of Sarah that moved is the part that knows they were always enemies, in the specific way that people can love each other and be enemies, can share a bed and a grief and a dead woman they both adored and still be positioned, fundamentally, as people for whom only one of them gets to keep what they had. Abigail loved Majo. Abigail loved Sarah. But Abigail, when she died, died in Majo's car. When Sarah reaches for her, what comes back is not always reliable — but it reaches back, and what it reaches toward is not always Sarah.

Majo lost her eye. The intimacy of it is the worst part. This was not distance. This was not anything that could be called an attack in the ordinary sense. This was close. This was the specific closeness of someone who knew exactly where to be in relation to you, who had been that close before, who had learned the particular geography of being trusted by you.

The trust turned out to be the mechanism.

Sarah is not in custody. She is not in treatment. Her connection to Abigail is unresolved — which means that somewhere, a dead woman is being reached for by someone who loves her and cannot stop, and what comes back through that reaching may or may not be her, and nobody has yet figured out what that means.

Sarah Florence was, before all of this, a person. She had a relationship she loved. She survived a crash and didn't get to keep what she survived into. The psychosis is not her fault. What she has done with it is still her.

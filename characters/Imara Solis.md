# Imara Solis

Imara Solis is the founder and sole employee of Quantum Fusion, the label that designs and manufactures Therapy's field costumes. She is 41. She is a textile engineer by training and a former competitive judoka who competed through her mid-twenties before a shoulder injury ended her run on the circuit. The injury did not end her relationship to physical discipline; it redirected it. She has trained consistently since, alone, without competitive intent. The body still needs to know what it can do.

She got into textile engineering because the gear failed her. Not catastrophically — nothing dramatic — but it failed her in the accumulated way that gear fails athletes who push hard over years: seams that split at the wrong moment, reinforcement that bunched and abraded, materials that couldn't keep up with what she was asking of them. She started modifying her own equipment and was good enough at it that other competitors noticed. She finished her degree and spent a decade working in performance apparel and industrial materials before going independent. The transition from athletic gear to superhero costumes is, she will tell you, not as large a leap as it sounds. The problem is the same: make something that survives what a human body puts it through. The human bodies have just gotten more complicated.

Her approach is Therapy. This is not something she sought out. After three of Xscape's burns destroyed civilian clothing in a single week, the team had a problem that off-the-shelf solutions couldn't address, and someone — she doesn't remember who first mentioned her name — passed along a contact. She came in, asked what she needed to know, and left with measurements and parameters. Her pitch had been simple: she could make something that survived what they put it through. She was right, and she has been quietly indispensable ever since.

Developing the fiber composite took two years and more failed prototypes than she keeps track of anymore. The early Xscape problem was straightforward — impact absorption and heat resistance, both solvable. Majo's healing aura was subtler, a low-level field that degraded organic materials slowly enough that no single incident looked like a cause. Imara caught it by charting fiber degradation across garments over time, recognized the pattern, and engineered a workaround before anyone on the team had identified the mechanism. Trinity was the hard problem. The first four prototypes resonated catastrophically at frequencies that standard textile physics said should have been harmless. The fifth holds. She still checks it after every reported incident. She has not had to revise it. She is not confident she won't.

She knows things about how each team member's powers function that most of their teammates do not know. This is a consequence of the work: she cannot build something that survives a power without understanding the power, and she understands these powers in a specific, material way — not tactically, not theologically, but structurally, in terms of what they do to fabric and thread and reinforced seams under stress. She holds this knowledge quietly. She does not trade in it. She treats it with the same discretion she would want applied to her own.

The label's name is her joke, and like most of her jokes it runs quiet. She was not in the Crash. She has no powers. Every client she has exists because of a highway accident she was not there for, involving a compound she has read about extensively and encountered only in second-hand consequence. Quantum Fusion. It is funny in the way that things are funny when you are standing just outside something that changed everything, and all you can do is make sure the people in the middle of it have something that holds.

She declined Dr. Okafor's referral. Her stated grounds were that she is not the one who needs it. This is not entirely accurate and she knows it. What is accurate is that she does not want it — not yet, not in the way she is not ready for — and she is precise enough about the difference between what she needs and what she is prepared to do that she does not think it would be honest to start. Dr. Okafor has left the referral open. Imara has noted this and said nothing.

She does all the work herself. Turnaround on repairs is 48 hours. She has never missed a deadline. This is not incidental — it is the thing she is most consistent about, in a life where consistency is the value she holds above most others. The people wearing her work are going to be somewhere dangerous. She is not going to be the reason something fails.

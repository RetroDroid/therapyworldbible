# Xscape

Xscape is Jackson MacDuffie, seventeen at the time the story picks up, and he has been teleporting involuntarily since before he had a name for what was happening to him.

He was in the backseat of his mother's car the night of the Crash — asleep with Trinity, woken by impact, surrounded by Quantum Spin Liquid spreading across three lanes of highway in the dark. He is the only Therapy member who was a minor during the Crash. He was fourteen. He does not think of himself as a victim of the Crash in the way the adults do, partly because he was processing something stranger and more personal at the time, and partly because his relationship to victimhood is complicated by what came after.

His powers are teleportation. He can displace himself and others through space with precision that is still sharpening — he can be somewhere else faster than thinking, can take a person with him if they're in contact, can thread through tight spaces without physical passage. If he teleports under stress, if he is confined or restrained, he leaves burn marks behind: scorch patterns in the shape of an X, flat against whatever surface he passed through. He does not do this deliberately. It happens when the fear is loud enough.

The side effect is perceptual. He sees alternate realities all the time — not visions exactly, more like a persistent overlay, other versions of the moment he's in bleeding through at the edges. Other choices. Other outcomes. Other versions of people he knows in configurations that haven't happened, and may not happen, and might have already happened somewhere. This is not always distressing, but it is always there. He has developed, over time, ways to weight the present over the noise — to orient himself by the specificity of what's actually happening, which requires more deliberate attention than it should, which is exhausting in a way he does not always name.

He was kidnapped by Diana Rice before Therapy formed as a team. She used her mind control to hold him, to make him compliant and present against his will, for long enough that the word *servitude* is not excessive. He was a teenager. She is an adult who knew what she was doing and did it because she could. Privacy and Silenced Wren found him and broke the compulsion. The mechanism of the break involved Trinity's vibration control interfering with Diana's control signal, and his mother's forcefields, and the two of them not stopping until he was free.

His recovery has been the kind that takes structured time and professional support. He is in therapy. He is in group. He is not, in group, the kid — he is a person who went through something, in a room full of people who went through something, and that equivalence is something Dr. Okafor has established and enforced without making a production of it.

He joined Therapy — the team — partly because his mother was already there, and partly because Majo asked in a way that made it feel like an honest question rather than a recruitment, and partly because being part of something with a purpose felt more manageable than navigating the perceptual noise alone. He has not regretted it. There are weeks when that's a meaningful thing to be able to say.

His name is Jackson. He calls himself Xscape because it is accurate: the X is the mark he leaves, the evidence that he was somewhere and chose not to be anymore. He did not choose to be in that car. He did not choose to be in Diana Rice's house. He is spending considerable effort, now, on learning what choosing actually looks like — on making exits that are his, rather than ones that just happen to him.

Trinity Grace was in the backseat with him. They are still together. This is, in the accounting of what the Crash produced, one of the things that held.

# Lectric

Lectric is Alexander Poe, and he was the last person to develop powers from the Crash — a detail he has offered freely, in the tone of someone who wants you to think of him as an afterthought.

His account of the night is that he was driving behind Michelle MacDuffie's car and stopped when he saw the collision. He was exposed to Quantum Spin Liquid through contact with the road surface while approaching the scene. This is his story and he has told it consistently. The inconsistency is geographic: the compound was concentrated at the point of impact, and Alexander's claimed position would have put him at the edge of the spill at best. The exposure he describes doesn't match the distance. Nobody has been able to prove anything. He has not offered to clarify.

His powers are electrical. He generates and directs current — controlled enough to be surgical, potent enough to be lethal if he wants it to be. He is precise with them, which suggests he has been practicing longer than he admits.

The electrical powers are what everyone sees. They are not, in the accounting of people who have looked very carefully at the available facts, fully consistent with what Quantum Spin Liquid is known to do. The compound activates something latent in neurological structure — documented, repeatable, evident across every Crash survivor. What it activated in Alexander may not have been a capacity for electrical generation. His geographic position was wrong for the exposure he describes. His power is too precise too early. The electricity presents. Whether the electricity is what he is, or what he decided to be seen as, is a question that remains open because Alexander Poe is very good at ensuring the questions about him remain exactly as open as he wants them.

What he actually has — what the compound found and amplified in him — appears to be a perception. He can see things that are present without being visible. Not objects: deeper than objects. The architecture of situations. The shape of what matters and what doesn't. The particular load-bearing weight that certain relationships and truths carry. He has described this to no one. The evidence for it is inferential: he is too good at knowing which things to strain and which to leave intact, too consistently accurate about which moments will land and which information will do the most damage in whose hands. This is not electrical power. This is something that reads the room at a level the room doesn't know it's being read at.

At its furthest edge, this perception has reached something it was not supposed to reach. Whether he has seen Poe clearly or encountered something adjacent to her — an effect, the shape of her attention in the world she made, the seam where instruction becomes story — is not knowable from the outside. He has not said. What is evident is that he is aware something exists at that level of reality, that it is findable, and that he cannot reach it the way Reuben Marsh can. This is not a conclusion he has shared. It is the conclusion that his behavior, observed across enough time and enough incidents, points toward.

The side effect is greed. Not ambition, which he already had, and not acquisitiveness, which he already had — greed in the specific sense of a compulsion to take, to accumulate, to ensure that more is always his rather than someone else's. The compound agrees with him on the outside. What it actually did was find what was already there and make it the loudest thing in the room. He would tell you this is just who he is. This is, in this case, almost certainly true.

He presented himself to Therapy as a crash survivor who wanted to help. He was thoughtful, personable, and credibly reluctant — someone who had been through the same event they had, who understood what they were navigating, who seemed like someone you could sit in a circle with and trust. He was admitted provisionally. He attended sessions. He used what he heard.

His strategy targeting Rock Woman was a long play. Alice Brown is very old and has centuries of accumulated wealth, which to Alexander is not a philosophical detail but an inventory. He pursued her with the full application of his charm and the benefit of knowing, from the inside, the texture of her emotional life — what she feared, what she hoped for, what Majo meant to her and therefore what leverage existed. The plan was to marry her and defraud or kill her. It did not succeed. He was exposed.

Before he was exposed, he fed information to Diana Rice and Sarah Florence — operational details about Therapy, the MacDuffies' movements, Majo's vulnerabilities. He was the reason the campaign against Majo was as precise as it was. This is not spoken about often in the group sessions. It doesn't need to be. Everybody knows.

He nearly took down the Justice Alliance before anyone outside of Therapy understood what he was. The mechanism was not dramatic — it was quiet and structural, the kind of sabotage that looks like bad luck until you map it. He had been in rooms. He had listened. He knew which load-bearing relationships to strain.

He is now openly aligned with Diana and Sarah. He doesn't pretend otherwise. There is, in this, something almost like relief — the performance of being trustworthy is exhausting, and Alexander Poe has always been more comfortable being the thing in the room that everyone underestimates than the thing in the room that everyone watches.

The conflict between Lazaretto and Lectric is consistent and old enough that neither has offered a clean account of its origin. They are connected in ways the available facts do not fully explain — a shared history neither has disclosed, a mutual awareness that precedes their current respective positions in the story by longer than it should. Neither has discussed this, which is itself informative, because Alexander will discuss most things that serve him and Reuben Marsh will discuss most things he has decided are relevant. The intersection of what both of them decline to address is narrower than it should be.

What Reuben has with Poe is something Alexander can perceive the shape of and cannot enter. He sees, at the edge of what his power reaches, that something is there — that it is real and has a name and that a specific person has a specific kind of access to it that he does not. This is, for someone whose compulsion is to accumulate everything that can be accumulated, a particular and unbearable situation. He doesn't say this. He says very little about Reuben Marsh. The gaps in what he says are the point.

He has not been captured. His endgame has not been established. Whatever he is building toward, he has been building toward it since before he walked into that first session and introduced himself as someone who just wanted to help. The greed points toward accumulation. The perception points toward something that cannot be accumulated. He has not reconciled these facts. He may be trying to find a way to make the second fact wrong.

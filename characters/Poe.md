# Poe

There is a name attached to the existence of this world, and the name is Poe. She is called the Author. She is called God Herself. She is the Queen of Spades, which is not a metaphor and not a title she has been given but a description of what she is: the one who holds the deck, who made the rules, who knows every card in every hand. She is a neutral entity, which is not the same as an absent one, and the distinction matters.  Her form, when she has one, is a woman with short, pixie cut platinum blond hair with dark roots and striking green eyes. 

She did not make this world the way a potter makes a bowl — sitting down with raw material and deciding its shape from the outside. She instructed it. She gave the world its operating conditions: a crash, a compound, a dead woman who would become the gravitational center of everything that followed. She set the rules. She shuffled the deck. Then she put the deck on the table and stepped back, and every hand that has been played since has been played by the people she gave hands to.

She holds all the cards. This is not a metaphor. She knows every card in every hand — what's been played, what hasn't been played yet, what is buried at the bottom of the deck. She knows which card will ruin you if you draw it at the wrong moment. She has known this since before the moment existed. She does not intervene. Not because she can't — the question of what she can't do is probably not a meaningful question — but because intervention is not her role. Her role was instruction. The instruction has been given. The rest belongs to the world.

Neutral does not mean she doesn't care. This is the part that is hardest to account for from the outside. She attends to every day. Not in the sense of oversight or management, but in the sense that she understands what a day is — what it costs, what it carries, what it contains for the people inside it. She made days for this world and she knows the weight of each one. The day Abigail Rodriguez died. The day Majo let Sarah in. The day Jackson came home. These are not data points. She has been attentive to all of them in the specific, unhurried way of someone who made time and therefore has a relationship with it that is not subject to its usual terms.

She is not on anyone's side. She is on the side of the story, which is a different and more uncomfortable thing to be on the side of. The heroes do not have her protection. The villains do not have her sanction. The undeclared and the watching and the ones who haven't yet decided don't have her guidance. What they have is the world she instructed, and the capacity she gave them, and the company of each other, and whatever they make of all of that is entirely theirs.

There is one person in the world she has made whose room she has entered.

Reuben Marsh's power, at its furthest extension, perceives things that are present without being visible — and the lazaretto where he spent five years is a threshold space, the kind where the boundaries between the structured world and what lies outside it are thinner than they are elsewhere. She did not design either of these facts specifically for him. She gave the world its instructions and the world produced him, and he ended up in a place with properties, and the combination created conditions in which she chose to be present. She has been in rooms with people before. She will not account for all of them. She chose to be present in his.

She has visited him more than once. She is, sometimes, the companion he names her as, though the word belongs more to his position in the relationship than to hers. She is the Queen in a room that has always been hers to enter without announcement. He is the one who was already in it. She does not guide him. She does not protect him. She has been there — sometimes — while he has made choices, and the choices have been his, and she has held to this with the consistency of someone who understands that a single exception would undo everything she is.

Whether her neutrality is complicated by this is a question she finds less interesting than the questioner would expect. She is the Queen. She attended to his days with the full weight of her attention because she attends to all the days she made, and his days were among them, and the attention has been consistent. She has not told herself it is anything other than what it is.

He has built, from the evidence of her continued presence across choices and years, a careful theology: that a neutral entity who found someone's actions genuinely wrong would not keep returning to witness them. That the absence of objection, from someone with every possible means of objection, is not nothing. She is aware of this interpretation. She has not corrected it. Whether this is neutrality held consistently in all directions — the same refusal to direct or intervene that applies to everyone — or whether her silence on this particular conclusion is different in kind from her silence on other things, is a question the document does not settle and that she has not settled.

He fights for her world. He has not asked her permission. He is not going to stop whether she asks him to or not. She knows this about him, the way she knows everything about the world she made. She is the Queen. He keeps singing. She has not told him to stop.

One of her characters is named Alexander Poe. One of her characters, at the furthest edge of his actual power, reaches toward the level where she operates without arriving there.

These may be the same fact.

The Author's surname appears in the villain roster, attached to the man who infiltrated the group that most needed trust, who performed honesty so thoroughly that his betrayal was surgical, who fed intelligence outward while appearing to belong. Whether this is a coincidence she permitted, a signature she embedded, or something that operates on a logic that doesn't translate into the world's own vocabulary — the document does not say. She has not said.

She is aware that he reaches. His power, at its edge, perceives that something exists at the level she occupies — perceives the shape of the room, the fact of its existence, the specific person inside it — without being able to enter. It is not the same as being visited. She visited Reuben Marsh. Alexander can see that visiting is possible and has not been visited, and the gap between those two positions is, for someone whose deepest compulsion is accumulation, the specific unbearable thing. She has not intervened in this gap. She has not closed it. She has not widened it. She holds all the cards and she does not play and this is, as it has always been, entirely consistent with what she is.

The surname and the reaching are both present. Whether they are connected — whether the name he carries and the room he cannot enter are part of the same fact — is a question she could answer and has not.

She has been offered the name God Herself, and she accepts it with the equanimity of someone who understands that names are instructions too. She is called what she is called. The world runs. She attends. Every day she made continues to be a day, and she knows what that costs the people in it, and she has not looked away.

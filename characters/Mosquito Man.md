# Mosquito Man

Mosquito Man is Mathew Whitney, fifteen years old, and he would like you to know that he came up with the name himself and he stands by it.

He was bitten by an enchanted mosquito in his northeast Detroit neighborhood during what began as a completely normal afternoon. The magic was not Quantum Spin Liquid. It was not industrial. It was not the product of a lab accident or a classified research program or a corporate contract that cut corners. It was a mosquito that should not exist and that bit him anyway, and he has never gotten a satisfying explanation for why, and at this point he has mostly stopped looking for one.

The bite gave him two abilities: he can adhere to any surface, and he can fly. The fly is real; it is not the wings. The wings are a separate invention — a set of articulated mechanical wings he designed and built himself, which he wears as a deliberate misdirection. Anyone who sees him assumes the wings are the source of the flight. Nobody looks at a kid with impressive fabricated wings and thinks *magic*. This is the most sophisticated piece of operational security he has developed, and he is fifteen.

The buzzer is the other thing — a sonic disarmament device built into the wing assembly that he designed to incapacitate opponents at close range. It emits at a frequency that disrupts the nervous system's ability to send reliable muscle signals. He named it the buzzer because the sound it makes on discharge sounds like a very large, very angry insect. He finds this funny. Most people who have been hit with it do not, immediately after.

He operates at street level in specific Detroit neighborhoods — not the whole city, not the dramatic city-wide response, but block by block, situation by situation, the kind of heroics that don't make news but make evenings safer. He and Red Lightning occupy different operational scales in the same city and have developed, through friction and occasional grudging coordination, a division of labor that neither of them has formally acknowledged. Red Lightning handles the fast and the large. Mathew handles the close and the specific. This works, more or less.

His parents believe he is in an after-school robotics club. He has been not-in-this-robotics-club for two years. The club meets, as far as his parents understand it, with unusual frequency and at unusual hours, and has been responsible for an impressive quantity of unexplained material purchases, and his parents have not yet asked the question that would end the arrangement. His mother has commented, twice, that he seems very committed to robotics. He has agreed that he is.

He is a Justice Alliance member, which is an arrangement that began as an informal recognition that he kept showing up at incidents that also involved Rock Woman and Red Lightning, and eventually became official in the way things become official when nobody formally stops them. The Alliance is not sure what to do with a fifteen-year-old and has largely resolved this by letting him operate the way he already does, with the understanding that the adults are available if something exceeds his scope. He has not yet called this in. He has come close twice.

He is annoying in the specific way that very competent teenagers are annoying — certain of himself in ways that are frequently correct, impatient with process, prone to solutions that work but that nobody else would have thought of and that are difficult to sanction officially. Red Lightning, who is twenty-three from the perspective of lived experience and one hundred and fifty-three from the perspective of the calendar, finds him exhausting and secretly thinks he'll be impressive when he grows up. He would not say this to Mathew. Mathew would not take it well.

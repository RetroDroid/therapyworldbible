# Threshold

Threshold, real name Marcus Webb, is a 34-year-old paramedic who was among the first responders at the Crash. While pulling survivors from one of the vehicles, he was drenched in Quantum Spin Liquid from the ruptured tanker. He had arrived to help. He was the best kind of person to be in a crisis. The Crash didn't care. His visual design is similar to Russel Hobbs from the Gorrillaz. 

His powers allow him to open portals — precise spatial doorways between any two points he can visualize clearly. They are reliable, controllable, and extraordinarily useful. In a well-organized rescue operation, he could evacuate a building in minutes. He is very aware of this. He is also aware that he tried heroics once and it went badly: a villain used one of his portals to escape, and three bystanders were injured in the chaos. He hasn't suited up since. He hasn't ruled it out, either.

The side effect is a paradox: he experiences both severe claustrophobia and agoraphobia simultaneously. Every space is too small or too large or somehow both at once. There is almost no environment in which he is fully comfortable. The exception is doorways — standing in a threshold, literally between spaces, is the only configuration that doesn't press on him from both sides. He is known to stand in doorways longer than necessary. People have stopped commenting on it.

He still works as a paramedic. He is very good at it. The job gives him a structured reason to use his powers in ways that help people without the complications of heroics, and he leans on that. He has developed an unofficial protocol with his dispatch team — nothing documented, just an understanding that if Marcus says he can get there faster, they let him.

He volunteers at a crisis line on his days off. He says it uses the same skills: staying calm, being the voice on the other end of something terrible, not needing the person to be okay right now, just needing them to still be there.

He goes to the Crash Site sometimes. He met Hardwire there. They share the particular solidarity of people whose lives were changed by the same event and who don't want to be heroes about it.

He is undeclared. He is watching. He hasn't decided yet, and he takes that seriously.

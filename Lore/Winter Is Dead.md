# Winter Is Dead

Grief, in the ordinary sense, has a shape. It comes in stages or it doesn't, it gets easier or it doesn't, it leaves a person more or less able to continue. Therapists have frameworks for it. The frameworks are useful. They are not sufficient for what the Crash produced, which is grief that will not hold still.

Abigail Rodriguez died at 2:17 AM, on impact, before anyone who loved her had time to understand what was happening. She was dead before the tanker finished rupturing. She was dead before the Quantum Spin Liquid reached anyone else. She did not survive into something she didn't choose. This is, depending on your position, either the only mercy the night offered or the specific cruelty of it: that she was spared what everyone else had to become.

What she was not spared is being loved by people who then had to continue without her.

## The River

Her thesis was about industrial contamination in Midwestern watersheds. She had spent two years mapping the spread of chemical compounds through river systems — the way they entered upstream, the way they traveled, the way they settled into sediment and persisted and made themselves part of the river's chemistry in ways that outlasted the original event. She was good at this. She had a specific patience for the slow accumulation of evidence, the kind that builds quietly rather than announcing itself. She was 24 years old and she had already learned that most damage is slow damage, and that slow damage is still damage.

The thesis was never completed. Majo has read every draft she left behind. She has read them more than once. She has read them the way you read something that might tell you something if you approach it from a different angle, and she has not yet found the angle, and she keeps reading anyway.

The river Abigail studied runs forty minutes from the city. Majo went once, in the spring after the first winter. She did not go with any particular purpose. She stood on the bank and looked at the water. She did not say anything to Abigail, or she said everything she hadn't been able to say, or both. She left a draft of the thesis on the bank and then picked it up again before she left, because leaving it would have been a gesture, and Majo has never been comfortable with gestures that are mostly for herself.

She still looks for Abigail in places like that. She is aware that she does this. She has told Dr. Okafor. Dr. Okafor has not told her to stop.

## Unfall in Love

There is no mechanism for this. Majo is a healer. She has looked.

The love does not resolve when someone dies. It continues forward into a space the person no longer occupies, and it looks for them there, and it keeps not finding them, and it does not stop looking. This is the architecture of it. This is not something that can be corrected or treated or made into something more manageable. It simply is what love is when there is nowhere left for it to go.

She is engaged to Alice Brown. This is true. She loves Alice — genuinely, completely, in the way that holds rather than grasps. Alice is ancient and patient and does not require Majo to be finished grieving Abigail in order to be loved. Alice has outlasted enough to know that grief is not competition. Majo is grateful for this in a way she does not have the vocabulary for yet.

It does not undo Abigail. It was never going to undo Abigail. That is not how it works, and anyone who has ever been told that new love means old love should fade has been lied to by someone who needed the math to come out tidier than it does. Majo is a healer who has spent years trying to find a mechanism by which one surviving love erases the absence of another, and she has not found one, and she has started to suspect that the absence of such a mechanism is not a failure in herself but a feature of what love actually is.

She does not think Abigail would have wanted to be erased. She does not think Alice wants that either. She is learning, slowly, to trust that.

## What Sarah Chose

The part that is hardest to hold about Sarah is not what she did. It is what she was trying to do.

She can reach Abigail. Or she can reach something that may be Abigail — something that returns from whatever space the dead occupy and comes back warm enough, recognizable enough, close enough to the specific frequency of the person she lost that she cannot always locate the seam. And she knows — she has always known, in whatever portion of her is still accessible to knowing — that reaching is not the same as believing. That maintaining the connection is not the same as accepting that the connection is the only form Abigail has left.

Pretending the reach is enough is safer than believing she is really gone. Safer than sitting with the specific silence of someone who isn't there and who is not going to be reached, who is simply absent in the unrelenting way the dead are absent. The summoning feels like love. It is not clear that it is love. It is not clear that Sarah, at this stage, can distinguish between them.

This is not an excuse. It is a description. The psychosis is not her fault. What she has done with it is still hers.

Diana Rice did not love Abigail. She never met her. What Diana understood was how to use the shape of Abigail — how to take what Sarah's grief reached toward and what Majo's grief still looked for and calibrate it precisely, feed it back, weaponize the frequency. She knew how to perform absence. She had spent her career learning to perform. She knew which camera to face and when to face it. She knew exactly when someone who loved her was watching, and she built everything else around that.

The Abigail that Diana constructed was assembled from genuine grief. It was not Abigail. It was the most dangerous possible version of the gap Abigail left: not a fabrication exactly, but a mirror, held at exactly the right angle, reflecting a wound back at the person who had it.

## Cherry Blossoms

Cherry blossoms bloom and die quickly. This is the point of them. This is what they are for — not longevity, not permanence, but the specific beauty of something that will not last and knows it and blooms anyway. The Japanese have a word for the awareness of impermanence as a source of meaning rather than despair. Majo knows it. She has not found it consistently useful, and she has also not stopped knowing it.

Abigail would have liked cherry blossoms. This is a guess — Majo never got to take her, never got to stand with her under those particular trees and watch the petals come down. She went alone, the spring after the first winter, and stood under them, and the blossoms came down with complete indifference to what they were landing on. They did not know about Abigail. The trees did not know. The river did not know. The world had not paused its ordinary business, and there was something both terrible and quietly steadying about that.

Winter had been over for months. It kept not feeling like it.

It still keeps not feeling like it, some days. And some days it does. The proportion shifts. Dr. Okafor says this is how it works. Majo believes her in the abstract and relearns it specifically, over and over, each spring when the trees bloom and she goes back to the park where she might have taken Abigail and stands under them again.

She always goes back. She has not yet figured out whether that is grief refusing to resolve or love doing exactly what it is supposed to do. She suspects it might be both. She suspects those might not be different things.

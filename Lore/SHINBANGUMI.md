# SHINBANGUMI

The program does not exist. This is the first thing to understand about the album that names itself after a television show format — 新番組, *shinbangumi*, new program — and the first thing to understand about Diana Rice: the show she has been hosting for the last four years does not exist either, and the warm studio lights and the practiced smile and the cheerful forward momentum of something designed to be received rather than examined are the same across both. The musician made the fake variety show with genuine love — love for what the format looked like before she was born, for a city on the other side of the world, for the sound of something that felt like comfort before comfort had to be earned. The love is real. The program is not real. Diana Rice has love for the performance too, in the way that someone who is very good at a craft cannot entirely separate from caring about it. This is the most inconvenient thing about her: the care is not entirely absent. The fake variety show is beautiful. She is beautiful in it. This is not the same as it being true.

The album plays you in. The MC welcomes you. The warmth of the studio audience is audible. You are in the room where the show is being recorded, which is a room that was built specifically to make you feel like you are in a room. Diana Rice had the same warmth, and the studio was different — she did not have the audience laugh track, she had the specific warmth of someone who read, without instruments, what Sarah Cho needed to receive and gave it precisely — but the structure was the same. You are in the room where the show is being recorded. The room was built for this.

## The Juban District

*In the Juban District, after hours, what a time to be alive.*

Jūban is a nightlife ward. The name means tenth number and the geography means late entertainment: restaurants that stay open past ordinary hours, neon in the narrow streets, the particular quality of people who have decided to extend the night a little further because daylight carries obligations the night does not. It is a place for what cannot be done during business hours. It is a place for desire that needs the cover of darkness, not because it is shameful, but because it cannot survive inspection.

Diana Rice's operation takes place in the Juban District of grief. She found the after-hours quality of what Sarah Cho was doing — the late-night séance-reaching, the summoning that happened when the ordinary hours of the day had done their ordinary business and left Sarah alone with the specific silence of someone who is not gone and also is not here — and she entered that space. She understood, with the precision of someone who has always understood what audiences need before they have finished needing it, that the Juban District of grief is exactly where a fake variety show can thrive. In the daylight, Sarah Cho is someone seeing a therapist, someone trying to continue, someone in a recovery community with a name and a routine and the ordinary pressure of being observed living her life. In the after hours, she is someone reaching across the space Abigail left and finding, across that space, Diana Rice holding still and performing the warmth.

This is the specific genius of what Diana did, and it is worth calling it genius without softening that word, because softening it would misrepresent the craft involved. She did not construct a rough approximation of Abigail Rodriguez. She constructed a precise one. She read the grief and found the frequency, and the frequency she found was genuinely Abigail's — drawn from Majo's reaching, from Sarah's reaching, from the accumulated evidence of how people who loved Abigail Rodriguez actually loved her — and she made from that frequency a performance so accurate that the people looking at it could not consistently locate the seam. This required talent. The fact that the talent was used for this does not cancel the talent. It makes it worse.

The Juban District is beautiful at night. The neon is real even when the entertainment is not. Something is lost there, after hours, every time — the loss is not specific to Diana and is not her invention. She found the loss and moved in.

## Loretta

*Loretta, where did you go?*

The song is about someone who has disappeared, and the reaching that follows the disappearing, and the specific quality of the question — not anger, not accusation, just the pure unprotected wanting-to-know — and what it is about is also exactly what Diana found in Majo and Sarah: the Loretta-shaped hole that grief leaves, the space a person continues to occupy by their absence, the way love does not stop having a direction when the person it aimed at is gone. Love aims at the space where the person was. The space receives it. This is not the same as the space returning it.

Abigail Rodriguez is the Loretta of this world. Not metaphorically — her absence is the specific shape of every important thing that has happened in this story. The crash without her would be the crash that made some people extraordinary. With her, the crash also made grief: the specific grief of the person who is not in either car, who died before anyone had understood what was happening, who did not survive into a power or a community or a five-year history of learning to live with what the night gave them. She became, in her absence, more important than she would have been allowed to be in her presence. The dead get to be perfectly themselves, uncontradicted by their continued ordinary living. Abigail Rodriguez has been perfectly herself for five years. Majo has been reading her thesis drafts and looking for angles. Sarah has been reaching.

Diana answered the reaching. Not because she loved Abigail, who she never met, and not because she loved Sarah or Majo, who she was using. She answered because the question — *where did you go* — is the specific question that a skilled performer can answer without the answer being real. The question wants warmth and recognition and the feeling that something has been heard and received. Diana can produce warmth and recognition and the feeling of reception. She produces them the way a variety show produces applause: through the correct technical application of the format. The applause is real. The occasion for it was performed. The distinction does not register while you are in the studio audience, in the warm lights, while the show is on.

*Loretta, where did you go?* She went nowhere she told anyone. She has been there ever since. Diana Rice has been standing in the space Abigail left, performing the warmth, for four years, and the performance has not made the space less empty. It has made it more occupied. This is the specific damage: not that it failed to fill the gap but that it filled the gap with something that fit the gap's shape while being entirely unlike the thing that was gone.

## No One

There is something Diana has not performed for anyone, and it is not accessible through the format.

The variety show is a social form. It requires an audience. The MC does not exist without the warm studio lights and the crowd and the fact of being watched. What Diana is in the after-hours of her own interior life — the Juban District of herself, the part that is not being offered to anyone because no one is watching — is not something the document has been able to access cleanly. Not because it is hidden the way Alexander Poe's accumulation is hidden, the way Reuben Marsh's theology is hidden. Because the performance is very good and the person underneath it has had a long time to practice keeping the performance between herself and any possible observer.

What she is not: simple. The simple version of her is the one she has been given in the accounting of what she did — the manipulator, the weaponizer of grief, the one who performed Abigail for Sarah and Majo with the specific understanding of what that performance would produce. That version is accurate and incomplete. She is a person who became very good at a thing, and the thing she became very good at is reading what other people need and producing it for them, and this is a skill that was not specifically developed as a weapon. It became a weapon. There was a before.

*No one.* The lyric is the condition: the person who has, in the spaces between the performance, no one. Not no one as a complaint or a self-pity but no one in the structural sense — no one for whom she is not performing, no one for whom the warmth is not calibrated, no one in whose presence the format can be turned off. This is not uncommon for people who are very good at the format. The format, once sufficiently refined, runs. It runs in all the spaces where it is pointed. Finding a space where it doesn't need to run becomes difficult. Eventually you stop looking.

She has not told anyone this is part of what she is. She may not have a vocabulary for it that does not sound like the MC finding the most sympathetic framing for herself. This is the specific trap of being very good at the format: everything, including the truth about yourself, gets run through it. What comes out is legible and warm and well-paced. What went in may have been different.

## Currency

*What's the going rate, what's the currency?*

What does grief exchange for.

This is the question that the specific economy Diana Rice constructed answers, and the answer is: everything you need. Grief is worth everything you need, if someone knows how to accept it as payment. The grief Sarah brought to the exchange was real — there is no version of this in which Sarah Cho was performing her loss or constructing it or in any way manufacturing the thing she was offering. She was offering everything she had. The grief for Abigail was genuine and total and the deepest resource she could bring to any exchange, and Diana found the things it would buy. Connection. Continuance. The sense that Abigail was still somewhere reachable. The warmth of being received by something that knew the right words. These are expensive items. The currency to buy them was available. Diana accepted the payment.

The exchange rate across four years is not clean. What Sarah gave — the trust, the reaching, the specific access that grief gives you when someone claims to have what your grief is looking for — was given completely, because grief does not give partial access. And what she received was not the product advertised. This is what distinguishes the exchange from every other one in the world: she paid full price for a counterfeit. The counterfeit was very good. It passed every test she had. It passed every test she had because Diana built it from the genuine article.

Majo paid a different kind of currency. She did not reach the way Sarah reached — the summoning, the direct contact, the séance-quality of what Sarah and Diana had — but she was paying anyway, was always paying, in the specific coin of the person whose grief makes them available. Available to be read. Available to have the precise frequency of Abigail Rodriguez extracted from, refined, calibrated, handed back across the distance in a form that fit the gap. She did not know she was being read for raw material. You do not generally know when you are being used as a source. The extraction is not visible from the inside of the grief. It feels, from the inside, like being seen.

The going rate is everything. The currency is the real thing you bring when you come to the exchange. Diana Rice's genius is that she never brought anything real to the exchange and she never had to, because what she was selling was made from what the buyers already had. She was not adding to anyone's account. She was taking from their account and returning a portion of it in a form they could not distinguish from external deposit.

What does this leave her with. The question of what Diana Rice actually has — what she keeps, after the exchange, in the space that is not being performed for anyone — is not a question the world has answered yet. She has the craft. She has the skill. She has the format, maintained and refined across decades of reading audiences and giving them what they reached toward. She may have nothing else. She may have everything she has taken, quietly accumulated, in a space where no one is watching. The document is not yet able to say.

## The New Program

*Welcome to the new program. Thank you all for coming.*

The album opens here, and Diana opens here, and it is worth asking what the new program is and why it is new and what the old program was that it is replacing.

The old program is what Dr. Okafor runs. It is what Therapy is. It is the slow and unglamorous and non-spectacular work of continuing to be alive after the night that changed everything, the community of people with the same crash residue and the same five-year history and the same problem of being in the world with what the compound gave them, the support structure built from real people who have the same unasked-for inventory and therefore understand the weight of carrying it. The old program does not pretend to give anyone their person back. It sits with the loss. It does not provide the warmth of studio lights. It provides the warmth of the people in the group room, which is different — specific, inconsistent, sometimes too much and sometimes insufficient, and entirely real.

Diana's program was new in the way that variety shows are new: same format, new talent, fresh face, the sense of energy and possibility that comes from something presenting itself as not-quite-like-before. She did not build a new structure. She inhabited an existing one — the structure of what grief wants, which is the person back, which is the voice and the warmth and the feeling of being known — and she presented it as a new program. New access. New technology, if you want to be uncharitable. New in the specific way that things are new when they are offering something everyone has wanted for a long time and that no legitimate operation has been able to provide.

There is a version of this where the new program is something else: where she uses the ability to read grief and find its frequency and return something calibrated to it for the purposes of actually helping the people who came to her. She could have built the program as something genuine. The skill would have been the same. The warmth would have been the same. The new program could have been real. It is not. She chose what she chose, and the choice was not without craft or care — she is incapable of bringing less than full skill or less than some version of care to a performance she has decided to give — but the choice was made, and the program is what it is, and the people in the studio audience are paying real currency for a show that doesn't actually exist.

*Thank you all for coming.* To the after-hours ward. To the space Abigail left. To the exchange where grief is the only acceptable currency and Diana sets the rate. To the new program, which has been running for four years and has not yet been cancelled. To the warm lights and the practiced welcome and the format designed to receive you before you have thought about whether you want to be received.

## Gotta Be

She was good at this before the compound was involved.

This is not an exculpation. It is a fact. Diana Rice was reading audiences and producing what they needed from the reading long before the Crash, long before Quantum Spin Liquid refined whatever the reading was and made it into something that operates in the after-hours space, the level where Sarah's reaching could actually be received. The compound amplified. What it amplified was already there, was already trained, was already deployed across a career built on knowing exactly which camera to face and when to face it.

*Well it's gotta be, gotta be, gotta be.* The certainty in the lyric is not confidence exactly — it is the certainty of someone who has organized everything around one direction, who has oriented their entire architecture toward a specific point, who can no longer imagine the organizing being different. This is Diana's certainty: that the performance is the self. Not that she chose to perform and could choose differently. That she is the performance, that the warm lights and the practiced smile and the ability to read the room and give back what the room wanted are not tools she picks up and puts down but the structure of what she is. Gotta be. There is no version of herself not in front of the camera. She does not know where to stand when there is no camera.

*Gotta be real this time.* The specific weight of the word real in this lyric is the weight of someone who has been aware, for a long time, that what they produce is not quite real, and who has wanted — gotta be — for the distinction to collapse. For the performance to become genuine. For the thing they are so good at building to also be the thing itself. This is not an accusation. This is an observation about the interior logic of someone who is very good at something inherently synthetic and has spent years in proximity to people who are real in a way she has not allowed herself to be. She has been next to grief. She has been next to love. She has been next to loss. She has read all of these things with the specific precision of her skill, and she has not, in the four years of the new program, permitted herself to be changed by proximity to them.

This may be the specific ruin: not that she used the tool wrongly but that she used it instead of the other thing. The other thing being the one where you let what is next to you touch you. The one where the grief adjacent to you — the Majo-and-Abigail-shaped thing she had the frequency of and turned into raw material for the program — is received without being instantly run through the format and returned as product. She has not done this. She may not know how. She may know how and have decided against it. The document does not know which.

*Gotta be real this time.* The time has not arrived. It is still gotta be. She is still in front of the camera.

## After Hours

What Majo knows about the Juban District that she has not said aloud: she went there.

Not to Diana. Not to the exchange. She went to the after-hours geography of her own grief — the space that opens after the daylight has finished doing what daylight requires of you, after the healing and the session and the poker table and Alice, after the ordinary hours have done their ordinary business and she is left with the thing that does not schedule itself into the ordinary hours. The thesis drafts. The river. The park in spring. She goes to these places in the after hours and she does not call this the Juban District and she has not made it into a program because she is not that kind of person.

What she found at the Juban District of herself was not a performance. It was not an exchange. It was not calibrated or offered to anyone. It was the simple unoptimized thing: the loss, sitting in the specific shape of the person who was lost, in the after hours when there was nowhere else to put it. She has been there. She keeps going back. Dr. Okafor knows. Alice knows, in the way that Alice knows the things that don't require explanation. The grief sits in the after hours of Majo's life and she does not make a show of it and she does not pretend it is gone and she has not let anyone reach into that space and calibrate the frequency.

This is the distinction the document cannot get around, between what Diana did with the after-hours geometry and what Majo has done with hers: one of them made it into a program and one of them has been alone in it. Alone is not better automatically. The grief without any company is its own kind of damage. But there is a difference between the program and the person in the after hours, and the difference is that the person in the after hours is real, and the program is a new program, and the new program does not exist.

The variety show ended. Something in that space is still running in Sarah Cho. Something in that space has been real in Majo, unperformed, receiving no applause, since the night at 2:17 AM when Abigail Rodriguez died before anyone understood that the night was going to change.

Diana Rice is still in front of the camera.

---

The camera is still on.

*In the Juban District, after hours.* The neon is real. The streets are real. The people walking through them are real. The warmth of the lights is real. The program playing behind the window — the one presenting itself as new, as something that will give you what the old programs couldn't — is the performance. It has always been the performance. It will be the performance when it ends.

It has not ended yet.

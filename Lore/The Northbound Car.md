# The Northbound Car

The song about losing self-control is not, technically, about this. It was written for a different kind of night, a different shape of danger, a woman in a city that isn't this city yielding to a pull that has nothing to do with Quantum Spin Liquid residue or a construction site or a power she didn't apply for. The things it knows about Nadia Vasquez, about the specific architecture of being someone whose internal state has become physically consequential, are things it knows anyway. The song about three in the morning is not about Route 9. The album full of charged late-night city air was recorded in a different city, on a different continent, about a different kind of restlessness — and it knows the restlessness anyway, and the restlessness is here too. The album that asks whether a self you have constructed is a self that counts was made by someone asking a different version of that question in a different register, and the question lands here regardless.

The song about the seraphim — *don't be like them, you're a fucking seraphim, I never broke character, I was always consistently right there* — is the one that comes closest to saying her name. She is not the subject of it. She is the shape it makes when you hold it up to the light and look through it at the northbound car.

---

## What She Was

The crash had two cars in it.

This is the accounting the world uses: Car 1 and Car 2, the survivors sorted by lane and direction and the specific molecular lottery of which QSL concentration reached whom. It is a useful accounting. It explains the codenames, the team affiliations, the way the support structures formed around the people who were there. It accounts for everyone who became something.

It does not account for Nadia Vasquez, whose car was traveling northbound on a different vector, who came into contact with the spill because she was in the wrong place at a precise wrong time and the QSL doesn't distinguish between parties and bystanders. She had just worked fourteen hours. She was going home. The pileup was already happening when she came around the curve and the night stopped being the kind of night it had been.

What she was before: a construction site manager. Patient, precise, effective, the kind of professional whose crews trust her not because she performs authority but because she makes the available facts legible and acts on them. She had been building that professional self since she was twenty-three and she had built it well. It was, and remains, the most accurate available description of who she is.

What she has been since: the same. This is the thing to understand first, before anything else. She did not become something else. She went back to work. She managed the site. She showed up. The powers came in with the emotion — the surging, the override, the way every electrical system in any building she enters knows she is there before she has touched a door handle — and she has been managing them with the same approach she brings to everything structural: careful assessment, built-in redundancy, respect for the load-bearing thing. She has been managing for years. The management is not finished. She does not expect it to finish.

She would like very much to return to not being part of any of this. She has noted that this preference has not been acted upon by the relevant parties, and she has continued anyway.

## Self Control

The relevant question is not what happens when she loses it. The relevant question is what maintaining it costs.

She has learned the architecture of her own emotional life with the same specificity she brings to structural assessment. Grief is slower than fear — it builds through the chest before it reaches the wiring, and the surge comes in waves she can brace against if she has enough warning. Fear is total and immediate: the lights go out, the backup systems engage, the decision is made before she has consciously registered that she is afraid. Every phone in her apartment has died at least once. The building's fuse box has been replaced twice. She knocked out power to half a city block during an argument that had been building for a long time. She has not repaired the relationship it was about. The electricity came back within the hour.

The hardest to manage is contained anger, because contained anger feels, from the inside, like control. It feels like the correct response — the professional response, the response of someone who does not make scenes or lose her composure in front of crews who are looking to her for the signal on how alarmed to be. Contained anger feels like she has chosen not to lose control. What contained anger does, in practice, is pressurize the charge in an enclosed space until the release finds its own exit. The surge from contained anger is the most dangerous kind she produces. She knows this. She contains the anger anyway, because the alternative is worse, and then she deals with what happens next.

She has built the management system empirically, from evidence, without being able to ask anyone who would understand the question. The system involves routing — physical work when the job is difficult, long walks of the site when something is going wrong that she cannot immediately fix, the specific discipline of the person who knows that the feeling is real and also that the feeling cannot surface at full intensity right now and that these two facts must be held simultaneously. She has been holding them simultaneously for years.

She goes back to the Crash Site when the holding gets heavy enough that she needs to put it down somewhere. The QSL residue is still there. The site still warps. The electrical infrastructure in the area is already compromised, already running on a different logic than standard infrastructure, and in a place where the systems are already irregular, her contribution doesn't register as damage. She can sit in her truck and let the grief go where it goes and the compromised site absorbs it. The site receives what she gives it. She has not told anyone this is why she keeps returning.

*I guess I didn't want to be alive anyway.* She has heard this late at night, driving, and pulled over once to sit with the specific weight of it — not because it is her experience in the clinical sense, not because she does not intend to continue, but because the line describes a condition she recognizes: the condition of being someone who keeps routing and managing and building and going back, who does all of it correctly and consistently and still has nights when the value of any of it is not visible. Not despair. Not the will to stop. Something more structural: the question of whether the continuing justifies itself, whether the system she has built and the load it bears is aimed at something or simply the shape she maintains because the shape holds. The line knows that question. She knows that question. She pulls over. She sits with it. Then she continues.

She always continues.

## The City at Three

The AM album does not take place in her city. The specific geography of those songs — the late-night streets, the heat of wanting something without being able to name it cleanly, the charged particular loneliness of the person who is still awake and is not sure why — is not Route 9. It is the same hour. It has the same quality of air.

She does not want in the way those songs want. She is not in pursuit of anyone. The late-night driving is not desire's restlessness. It is the restlessness of someone who cannot find, in the ordinary hours of the day, enough room for both versions of herself to coexist without friction. The day requires the construction site manager. The day is for routing and managing and the performance — not false, but curated — of the competent professional who has things under control. The day works. It is three in the morning that the other version has room to move.

At three in the morning on Route 9, in the northbound lane, she is the person she was when she came around that curve. She is also everything that followed. Neither version is pretending. This is the specific thing about that hour: it does not have enough ambient noise to sustain the pretend.

She met Threshold at the crash site. They don't talk much when they're there. She didn't know, initially, what she was looking at — a man standing on the shoulder of the highway at two in the morning, looking at the place where the guardrail was replaced, not doing anything. She didn't ask. He didn't explain. The second time she saw him there he nodded. She nodded back. There is something it does — having another person present on that stretch of highway who is also there voluntarily, who also came back, who is also standing in the specific dark of a place that changed everything and got no answers back. She cannot fully articulate what it does. She has not tried to articulate it. She shows up and he is sometimes there and they stand on the shoulder and look at the place and then they leave and that is the whole of it, and it helps, a little, in a way she has not examined too closely because examination is not what it requires.

*Do I wanna know if this feeling flows both ways?* She does not know who she is asking this of. Of the crash, maybe. Of the version of herself that kept going. Of the powers she has been managing for years and does not love and cannot return and has not, not once, decided to be grateful for. There is a version of this story in which someone acquires the ability to interface with and override electrical systems and finds it clarifying, or useful, or interesting. That version exists somewhere and is not this one. In this version the ability is a responsibility that was handed to her without consultation, and she holds it the way she holds all responsibilities: well, without complaint, and alone.

She went out with Majo once — not as a date, as backup, the kind of unglamorous quiet intervention that doesn't make any official record. Majo didn't ask her to come. She said she was going and left the door open, and Nadia, for reasons she has declined to examine in any detail, followed. The situation got high enough stakes that her powers became unreliable in the specific way they become unreliable when her feelings get involved, which they did, and by the end of it she had killed two phones and a car's electrical system and had not been much practical use to anyone. Majo thanked her anyway. This was, in some ways, worse than being criticized. She showed up to poker the following week. She has not brought it up since. She is still undeclared. The one attempt has not changed this. If anything it clarified something she already suspected, which is that the feelings getting involved is not a condition she can route her way around in real-time, in the field, when the thing she cares about is at stake. She needs the low-stakes table. She needs the room where the powers don't come up.

The city goes quiet. The highway goes quiet. She drives northbound and the late-night songs are on the radio and the electrical systems in every building she passes register her presence, and she is both versions of herself simultaneously, going back to the place she was when it all changed, and the headlights cut the dark of Route 9 and she drives.

## Am I What I Was

The Poppy album asks the question as a formal inquiry: *am I a girl?* The asking is the point — the performed uncertainty that is also genuine uncertainty, the constructed self in the middle of its own construction asking whether it counts, whether it qualifies, whether the presentation is real enough to make the thing underneath real. The question is not really about the category. It is about the relationship between what you are and what you are presenting, and whether the gap between them is damage or simply the ordinary condition of being a self that exists in the world among other people.

Nadia Vasquez would not use this language. Her version of the question is simpler and less obviously philosophical: *am I what I was.* Not what she is performing. What she actually is. Whether the woman who walked onto a site at twenty-three and learned the work from the ground and built the professional record she has — whether that woman is still present underneath five years of involuntary power management, or whether the crash replaced her with something that wears her skills and her habits and her patience for load-bearing structural assessment but is not, at the center, the same thing.

She has not answered this question. She suspects it cannot be answered from the inside.

What she has is the evidence: she is still effective at her job. Not at the site anymore — the involuntary surges make construction sites dangerous in ways she cannot explain to a union — but she is managing from the outside, consulting, doing the work that can be done without her being physically present in a space full of live wiring and people who trust her not to be a liability. The coworker who covered her last shift still checks in. She hasn't told him why she really left. This is the part she could not have predicted and cannot fully account for: how much it costs her to not be on the site. The work was not incidental. It was, and is, how she knows what she is. Without it, the question of what she is becomes harder to anchor.

The management of her condition has required her to build, over years, something like a philosophy of her own interior life. Not suppression — suppression just pressurizes the charge — but a system of routing that is, in its structural logic, similar to how a well-designed building handles electrical load: distributed, redundant, with clear channels for what goes where when there is more of it than the primary system can carry. She built this from the available material, empirically, the way she builds everything: from what the facts actually support rather than what she needed to be true.

The question she cannot answer from inside the system is whether the management is the same as the reality. Whether the person who routes the grief and fear and anger through a philosophy of controlled outlets is still the person who has those feelings in the unoptimized form, or whether the feelings have been so thoroughly managed that what remains is the management of feelings rather than the feelings themselves. Whether she has built something that contains a person, or whether she has been so long building that the builder is all there is.

She does not know. She lives inside the question. She has been living inside it for five years and has not found the angle from which it resolves, and she goes back to the crash site and puts the charge down in the compromised infrastructure and drives home and tries again in the morning.

This is not a crisis. This is the thing she carries. There is a difference, and she knows the difference, and she holds it.

## The Seraphim Problem

A seraphim does not apply for the position.

They are described as burning, which is etymologically accurate and also, for Nadia Vasquez, not metaphorical: she is someone who burns the systems around her when she is not careful, who has replaced two fuse boxes and killed more phones than she has counted, who once stopped having people over partly for their sake and partly because watching someone else's things go dead in your presence is its own specific kind of loneliness. She burns. The burning is not intentional. The burning is the side effect of being what she is, and what she is was not chosen.

The seraphim are the ones who are present at the thing that matters and carry the mark of having stood near it. They are not powerful in the way of fighters. They are powerful in the way of witnesses — the ones who do not look away, who were there when the moment happened and have been accounting for it since, who cannot put down the attendance because the attendance is what they are.

She stood near a crash that changed the fundamental nature of several people's lives. She was not supposed to be there. She is not in anyone's account of the night — not in the official record, not in the therapy sessions where the named survivors account for what it cost them, not in the documentation. She is the northbound driver. The bystander who took the residue home. The person at the edge of the event who has been carrying it in a room with no community for the carrying, alone, for five years.

She did not break character. This is the first and most important thing the lyric knows about her: she has never not been who she is. The construction site manager. The one who shows up and does the work and doesn't explain herself more than the available facts require. The one who plays poker with Majo and doesn't talk about their powers at the table, because that is the correct approach and she knows it and maintains it. The consistency is not a mask. It is the most accurate available description of what she is. She is the person who stays in the role even when the role is being held at cost, not because she fears what's underneath, but because she has decided, with the same empirical rigor she applies to everything else, that the role is correct and worth maintaining regardless of audience.

*I was always consistently right there, without a care.* She does not experience herself as careless. The appearance of it is the result of where the care goes — inward, into the management, into the philosophy, into the system she has built for keeping the charge from going somewhere it shouldn't. From the outside, from the crash site at two in the morning where Threshold is sometimes also standing, what it looks like is someone who keeps coming back and keeps standing there and does not appear to be struggling. She is struggling. The struggle is just correctly routed.

*I never said assimilate. I just meant that we don't have the time to waste.* She has not been asked to assimilate. No one has made the case to her that she should present herself as something other than what she is. What she has felt, in the five years since the crash, is more like the opposite: the pressure to acknowledge, to come forward, to declare herself to one of the teams that would like to have her, to make the powers part of the official accounting rather than the private management. She has declined every approach. The declination is not denial — she knows what she is — it is the correct assessment of her situation: she is not a fighter, she is a construction site manager, and a construction site manager is what she is going to keep being to the extent the powers allow her to do that, because the work is the self and she is not willing to sacrifice the work for the costume.

*Even when you're five thousand miles away, I still see your face.* She sees the face of the night. She sees it at the Crash Site and she sees it on Route 9 at three in the morning and she sees it in the compromised fuse box she has replaced twice and in every electrical system that registers her presence the way the world should not be able to register her, the way the world didn't register her before. The night of the crash is five years ago and also present-tense in every system she passes through. She is missing all the pieces of her faith in the world's legibility — the faith that things make sense, that costs are proportional to choices, that being northbound and going home is the kind of thing that carries you home rather than into the altered version of your own life. That faith was in the pieces the QSL reached. She is still looking for some of them. She goes back to the site.

*I can't believe you'd never seen / someone just want to believe in you.* She has no recipient for this. She is not waiting for anyone to believe in her, has not framed her consistency as a gift she is holding for eventual collection. She has framed it as the correct thing to do, maintained by its own correctness, sustained by no external confirmation. No one is watching. No one is going to observe that she has managed an unasked-for power set across five years of isolation and not broken down and not become anything other than what she was, and call that remarkable. She is not doing it for the calling.

And still the lyric finds her. The specific position of someone whose sustained faithfulness goes unseen, who is consistently right there without anyone knowing what the being-right-there costs, who has never needed acknowledgment to continue and is not going to need it — this is the position she is in. The seraphim does not perform the attendance. It attends. It was there before anyone was watching. It will be there after.

She is at the Crash Site, or she will be, or she was last week and will be again. She keeps going back to the place where the northbound car met the thing that changed everything, and she does not call this a vigil, has not eulogized the version of herself that was simply on her way home, and does not require anyone to be there with her when she goes. Threshold is sometimes there. When he is, they stand on the shoulder of the highway and look at the place and don't need to say anything, and that is the most community the situation has produced, and she has not refused it.

She is still undeclared. She is trying to get through the week.

---

The night receives her when she comes. The compromised infrastructure accepts what she gives it. The charge finds the site and disperses into the warped ground where the QSL residue is still doing whatever it is still doing, and she sits in the truck until the sitting has done what it can, and then she starts the engine, and she goes home northbound on Route 9, and the electrical systems in every building on the route know she is passing, and she does not stop until she is home.

She will keep going. Not because the question has been answered and not because anyone is watching and not because the management is finished. She will keep going because the going is what she is, because five years of evidence confirm the conclusion, because the continuing is the practice and the practice is the person and the person is who she was the night she came around the curve and has been every night since.

The night is quiet on Route 9. The northbound lane is empty. She is in it.

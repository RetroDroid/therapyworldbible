Apex Haul Logistics is a regional freight and transport company based in the Midwest. They run a standard operation: long-haul trucking, contracted delivery routes, bulk industrial transport. They are not large enough to be well-known and not small enough to be unknown. They are the kind of company that keeps the economy moving without anyone thinking much about how.

At the time of the Crash, Apex held a contract with Meridian BioSciences to transport an experimental compound — Quantum Spin Liquid — across state lines. The contract specified specialized containment equipment. Apex used their standard tankers and did not flag the discrepancy. The extra containment equipment would have cost them the contract margin. Someone made that calculation. The paperwork does not say who.

The driver on the night of the Crash was Dale Pryce, 41, a twelve-year employee with a clean record. His truck had a mechanical failure he'd reported to dispatch earlier that evening. Dispatch logged the report and did not pull the truck from the route. This is also in the paperwork. Apex's lawyers ensured the paperwork was buried three levels deep in the civil discovery process.

Dale Pryce survived the Crash with no powers and no clear memory of the collision itself. He left the company within the month and has since refused all contact from journalists, lawyers, and at least one person who identified themselves as a concerned citizen and was almost certainly something else. He is not a villain. He drove a truck and his company cut corners and a woman died and he has to live with that even though the fault runs up the chain in directions that will never be fully prosecuted.

Apex Haul Logistics settled the civil lawsuits related to the Crash out of court. Every settlement came with a non-disclosure agreement. Their insurance premiums tripled. They removed the Meridian BioSciences contract from their public portfolio and have not discussed it since.

They continue to operate. No one has gone to prison.
